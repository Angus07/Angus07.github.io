Lately, one phrase keeps showing up: harness engineering. In the world of LLMs, it points to a simple truth: when you work with a model that is powerful but unstable, output quality is often determined less by the model itself than by the scaffolding around it: rules files, tools, memory, verification, control flow, and task orchestration. **Put the same model in two different environments, and it can behave like two different species.**

I think the same judgment applies to human beings. We like to explain someone's productivity, clarity, or consistency in terms of willpower, talent, or personality, as if those were purely internal traits. But from a more sober angle, **the human mind is also a powerful system that is noisy, easily polluted, and highly sensitive to context. The real gap between people is often not who has the better brain, but who has built the better surrounding system.**

Human beings are far more complex than models. We have bodies, emotions, memories, histories, and values. But that is exactly why we are so easy to derail. Sleep, mood, social pressure, short-term stimulation, and environmental friction can all hijack the mind from the outside. **Our biggest problem is rarely a lack of capability. It is the inability to call on that capability reliably.**

What I often see is not that smart people are not smart enough, but that their context is contaminated. Notifications, fragmented input, unfinished tasks, vague goals, and casual commitments all run like background processes, quietly consuming working memory. From the outside, a person still looks like they are sitting at the desk and working. On the inside, the cognitive workspace required for judgment and creation has already been depleted. **What looks like a failure of focus is often just a failure to clear the workspace in the first place.**

What follows from this is simple: a person needs a harness of their own. If we borrow the main elements from harness engineering, the first one is rules.

## Rules

For an agent, rules live in `AGENTS.md`. For a person, they look more like a system prompt written for the self: Who am I? What am I really trying to build? What do I value? What kind of qualities do I want to embody? What principles do I refuse to compromise on? **A personal `AGENTS.md` is a way of reminding yourself, over and over, of your identity, mission, values, and direction before the world starts rewriting them for you.**

In practice, I think it is worth rereading that personal `AGENTS.md` every morning. Not because ritual is sacred, but because orientation matters. Without a few explicit rules, everything becomes a case-by-case decision, and case-by-case decision-making is where mood, pressure, and other people's urgency take over. **The point of rules is not moral performance. It is to reduce decision load and protect the quality of your attention.**

## Memory

The second component is memory. One of the mind's favorite illusions is the belief that important things will not be forgotten. Reality works differently. A half-formed insight, a new judgment, a method that actually worked once: if it is not externalized, a few days later it often survives only as a weak feeling that you had a thought about it. **The point of memory is not simply to preserve the past. It is to provide usable context to your future self.**

That is why I care so much about logs, work notes, project journals, question lists, and the small reviews that can look almost trivial from the outside. They are not decorative habits. They are external memory for the cognitive system. Many people do accumulate experience, but because it never gets turned into something searchable, inheritable, and composable, they cannot really reuse it. **Without external memory, growth keeps resetting to zero at exactly the moments when continuity matters most.**

## Skills

The third component is a skill library. Experience that remains at the level of "I more or less know how to do this" is still expensive and fragile, because it depends too much on state. It only becomes durable when it is turned into checklists, templates, steps, heuristics, and standards. **One of the hidden advantages of high performers is not simply that they know more, but that they have packaged useful experience into reusable skill modules.**

That is why I have become less romantic about improvisation. Improvisation matters, but long-term output is usually supported by prepared structure, not bursts of inspiration. We need to keep extracting methods from experience, summarizing what works, and turning those patterns into workflows we can actually reuse. **Stability is what happens when good outcomes stop being accidents and start becoming probabilities.**

## Tools

Then there are tools. There is a reason the old saying about sharpening your tools still endures. If a tool materially extends your thinking or reduces friction, it is often worth paying for. With frontier models, for example, I would rather use the best one available. The mistakes, wasted cycles, and rework caused by weaker tools can easily cost more than the subscription itself. In the AI era, the scarcest resources are attention and time. **When you can use the best tools, your leverage often compounds rather than improves linearly.**

## Verification

Then comes verification. People are especially prone to overestimating their own sense of progress. Busyness can masquerade as momentum. Familiarity can masquerade as understanding. Planning can masquerade as action. Without external checks, it is easy to keep working hard subjectively while remaining objectively stuck. **A surprising amount of stagnation is not a talent problem but a feedback-loop problem.**

That is why important goals need to be made verifiable. Writing should be tested against whether a piece is actually publishable. Learning should be tested against whether something can be explained clearly and used in practice. Training should be tested against numbers. Projects should be tested against something the outside world can actually see, use, or evaluate. Verification is not about creating pressure. It is about removing fantasy from the system. **A mind without verification is unusually vulnerable to confusing narrative with reality.**

Verification is also the foundation of goal management. It helps to design a few hard checkpoints in advance, so that you can test whether you are actually moving toward the goal and executing against the plan rather than merely feeling as if you are. **If a goal cannot be checked, it is dangerously easy for it to become a story rather than a direction.**

## Control Flow

The last component, and maybe the most overlooked, is control flow. I have increasingly noticed that many problems do not come from difficulty itself, but from mixing incompatible modes of work inside the same context: trying to conclude while still exploring, continuing to diverge while supposedly deciding, interrupting execution with fresh input, or bringing emotional self-defense into review. **Cognitive quality drops when states and roles are not cleanly separated.**

I now prefer to think of myself as a system that needs scheduling, not as a heroic subject who should be able to perform at full power on demand. Some periods are for input and observation. Some are for writing and building. Some are for editing and review. And sometimes the most important move is to deliberately clear context: take a walk, mute messages, change rooms, and let the residue of one task stop leaking into the next. **What we call self-control is often just the ability to redirect attention with intention.**

There is an old Zen story about a monk being asked what he did before enlightenment. "Chop wood, carry water, cook rice," he says. And after enlightenment? "Chop wood, carry water, cook rice." So what changed? Before enlightenment, while chopping wood he was thinking about carrying water; while carrying water he was thinking about cooking; while cooking he was already inside the next task. After enlightenment, chopping wood was just chopping wood. Carrying water was just carrying water. Cooking was just cooking. **Read through the lens of context management, the story is really about clean isolation between tasks. The old monk had effectively split himself into three subagents.**

## Conclusion

Put together, this is what "harness engineering for the human mind" means to me. It is not about turning a person into a machine, and it is not a celebration of optimization for its own sake. It is a serious but humane form of self-design: accepting the limits of the mind, respecting the force of environment, and using external structure to protect internal ability. **Mature productivity is not about burning yourself harder. It is about making yourself callable, over the long run, at a high level of quality.**

I increasingly suspect that the future gap between people will not be defined first by who can grind longer or push harder. It will be defined by who realizes sooner that trying to fight a high-noise world with a bare mind is an inefficient way to live and work. The people who keep producing judgment, output, and results usually have an invisible support structure behind them. **They are not necessarily stronger by nature. They simply started earlier on the work of building a system around their own mind.**
